AI for Lawyers: the Agent Drafts, the Lawyer Decides

Legal work is documents, and much of it repeats: standard contracts, checklist reviews, searching the archive, forms. An AI agent takes that routine and returns lawyers’ time to what needs judgment: risk assessment, negotiations, non-standard cases. Every agent output is checked and approved by a lawyer. That is a principle, not an option.

Pilot on one contract type Ask a question

What AI can do in legal work

An AI agent for lawyers is a program that does preparatory document work by rules set by your lawyers: reviews contracts against checklists, searches the document base, prepares standard forms, and watches deadlines. Decisions stay with people.

First-pass contract review

The agent walks a contract against your checklist: mandatory terms, party details, deviations from the standard form, disputed wording. The lawyer gets a marked-up document with comments.

Document-base search

Questions like “which contracts with this counterparty are active” or “where did we already approve this wording” get answers in minutes instead of manual folder hunting.

Standard forms

The agent fills standard documents from templates. Data is pulled from your systems; the lawyer checks the result.

Deadline control

Contract endings, response deadlines, renewals. The agent reminds in advance, not after the fact.

Summarization

Short summaries of long documents and correspondence: substance, parties, obligations, risks. That speeds immersion into a matter.

What the agent does not do

We state applicability limits plainly because we work with a professional audience. The agent does not give legal opinions, does not assess dispute prospects, does not sign or send documents without a human, and does not replace a lawyer in negotiations or court. Responsibility for decisions rests with the lawyer; the agent prepares material for the decision.

How many hours it saves

Legal work is document work, and AI effects in document workflow are measured in an industry report: document processing cost falls 70–80%, manual-entry errors by 90%, document audit speeds up 35–40%. In hours it looks like this: a first-pass review of a standard contract takes the agent minutes, and a specialist half an hour or more.

How confidentiality is ensured

For legal work this is question number one. Models run inside the company perimeter or on Russian hosting; documents do not leave to external services. Agent access is limited to assigned folders and document types; every action is logged. If company data is still scattered across folders and systems, we help put data in order before the pilot. Requirements of 152-FZ (Russia’s personal-data law) and trade-secret rules are built into the architecture from the start.

Pilot on one contract type

  1. Choose one contract type. The highest volume: supply, services, NDA.
  2. Formalize the checklist. Your lawyers lock review rules; the agent works by them.
  3. The agent prepares drafts. The lawyer checks every result and edits; edits go into the knowledge base.
  4. Measure hours before and after. The expansion decision is made on your department’s numbers.

Compliance scenarios

For companies with regulatory load, the agent tracks change: monitoring new requirements, reconciling internal policies, preparing updates with an audit trail of “what, when, and why changed.” This pattern came from financial services and transfers to any regulated business.
