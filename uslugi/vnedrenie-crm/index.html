Why a business needs CRM

Sales channels keep multiplying: online commerce grew 32% year over year, and requests are scattered across messengers, email, and phone. Without one system, part of the inquiries simply never reach a manager in time.

CRM solves this with mechanics, not slogans. Every inquiry from any channel automatically becomes a deal with an owner and a deadline; client history lives in the card, not in a departed manager’s head; and the pipeline shows where sales stall.

What end-to-end analytics is

End-to-end analytics is reporting that links channel spend to deal revenue: how much a lead from each source cost and how much money it brought. With it you see that a lead from SEO closes cheaper than an ad lead, while “cheap” leads from some channels never reach payment. How to choose a service, what implementation costs, and why CRM disorder makes reports lie: on the separate end-to-end analytics page.

CRM + AI

Clean CRM data is fuel for AI. On that base, modules with measurable effect work: AI lead scoring lifts win rate by up to +76%, and manager productivity rises 30–50% because AI writes the call summary into the card and sets the next step.

Why 30–50% of CRM implementations fail

According to industry reviews, 30–50% of CRM projects miss their goals. The reasons repeat: rolled out without measurable goals, processes were not described, data was dirty, managers sabotaged and went back to Excel, the project had no owner.

Our safeguards match each reason: goals and metrics lock at audit, processes are described before setup, the database is cleaned at migration, training runs on live deals, and for the first weeks we control discipline together with the manager. CRM dies not from bad setup but from manager silence in the first two weeks, so control is part of the project, not an add-on.

How implementation works

  1. Audit. Channels, processes, loss points. Takes 1–2 weeks.
  2. Setup. Pipelines, fields, permissions, automation.
  3. Migration. We move the base from Excel or an old CRM, with deduplication.
  4. Policies. We lock what a manager must enter and when.
  5. Training. On real deals, not slides.
  6. First-weeks control. Discipline and fine-tuning from actual scenarios.

From practice. The fastest way to kill CRM is to turn on every module at once. We turn sales on first; the rest as the team adapts. The typical “everything at once” mistake tops the list of failure causes.
