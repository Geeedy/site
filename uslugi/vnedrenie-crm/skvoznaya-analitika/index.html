End-to-End Analytics: Which Channel Makes Money and Which Burns It

Metrica shows clicks and visits; money comes from deals. Between click and payment a lead passes through the website, CRM, and sales, and without end-to-end analytics that path stays a dark corridor: budget is allocated by feel. We implement end-to-end analytics turnkey together with order in CRM, without which any reports lie.

What end-to-end analytics is in plain language

End-to-end analytics is a reporting system that links ad spend to revenue from deals. It unites data from ad accounts, the website, CRM, and cash into one chain and shows the customer path from first click to payment, including repeat sales.

How end-to-end analytics works

The mechanic has four links. Ads pass UTM tags and spend. The website records the visit and turns a visitor into a lead via a form, chat, or call with call tracking. CRM moves the lead through the funnel to deal and payment. The analytics system glues it all into a report where each channel shows cost per lead, cost per deal, and return on investment. A break in any link ruins the picture, so implementation is not “install a counter” but build a complete data chain.

What it shows: metrics

Main indicators: CPL (cost per lead), CPO (cost per order/deal), ROMI (return on marketing investment), and LTV (lifetime value of a customer). A typical first-month insight looks like this: the channel with the cheapest leads brings applications that never reach payment, while an “expensive” channel brings deals with better economics. Without end-to-end analytics that channel would have been turned off for being expensive.

Four maturity stages of analytics

  1. UTM + CRM report. Free: you tag campaigns and view deal sources in CRM. For one or two channels that is enough, and you do not need end-to-end analytics yet.
  2. Metrica with CRM data. Yandex’s free path: goals, spend upload, CRM and offline-conversion data transfer.
  3. End-to-end analytics service. Roistat, Calltouch, and peers: automatic glue across all accounts and call tracking.
  4. Turnkey implementation. Service plus CRM order, manager rules, and training. Our work.

Honest takeaway from the ladder: if ad spend is under roughly 100,000 RUB per month and there is one channel, start with stages 1–2 for free. Services and implementation pay back across several channels and meaningful budgets.

Why analytics lies without CRM order

End-to-end analytics takes money data from CRM. If managers create deals intermittently, lose sources, and close sales after the fact, the report will show a pretty lie. Per industry analyses 30–50% of CRM implementations miss their goals, and on such CRM end-to-end analytics is meaningless. So we do both parts: we configure analytics and put funnel order in place: logging rules, required fields, oversight in the first weeks.

Effects in numbers

Per aggregated analytics-service cases for 2023–2025 (practice data, not official statistics): reallocating budget from non-paying channels cuts ad spend 15–30% without revenue drop; cost per lead falls 20–35% over 3–6 months; share of non-target leads drops to 20–40%; and lead-to-sale conversion rises 10–25% thanks to transparent manager work.

Attribution models: which to choose

Attribution is the rule for which channel gets credit for a deal when the customer touched several. Last click is simple and fits short sales cycles; first click values awareness channels; a linear model splits value across touchpoints. For a start, last click with a manual check of long deals is almost always enough; complicate the model only when the team trusts the base figures.

Implementation stages

  1. Audit. Channels, tagging, CRM state, Metrica goals. We find breaks in the chain.
  2. Service selection for channels and budget, with total cost of ownership.
  3. Link setup. Ad accounts, call tracking, site forms, CRM.
  4. CRM order. Logging rules, required fields, funnel shaped for analytics.
  5. Launch and calibration. First month we reconcile reports with reality and teach the team to read the numbers.
