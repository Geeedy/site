Business Process Audit and AI Strategy: We Calculate Where AI Pays Back

Classic consulting describes your processes and leaves a pretty report. We audit differently: we calculate what each process costs in hours, rates, and errors, and show where artificial intelligence pays back and where it does not. The audit ends not with a presentation but with an AI strategy with priorities and a savings calculation. That is the first step of any AI implementation, and you can start free, with one process.

What a business process audit is

A business process audit is a systematic breakdown of how the company does work: who does what, in what sequence, and at what cost. The audit finds bottlenecks, duplicates, and waste, and in our version also answers the main question: which processes are worth handing to AI and how much that saves.

When a company needs an audit

Across aggregated surveys of Russian companies, up to 30–50% of employee time goes to routine: manual data entry, hunting for information, duplicating operations across systems, and email approvals. You need an audit if you recognize yourself in at least one of five situations:

  1. You plan automation or AI and do not know which process to start with.
  2. The company is growing, but processes stayed from when headcount was half as large.
  3. You already tried automation, but the pilot gave no effect and was shut down.
  4. Processes live in people’s heads, and one employee leaving stops a department.
  5. Costs grow faster than revenue, and it is unclear where team hours go.

The motivation gap is simple. A consultant who does not implement does not own the feasibility of recommendations. We implement AI ourselves, so only what we are ready to deliver and prove with metrics enters the strategy.

Method: four steps from map to strategy

  1. Process map (AS IS). Team interviews and system review. We document how work runs now, in standard BPMN notation.
  2. Economics of each process. We calculate by formula: employee hours × rate + cost of errors. Each process gets a monthly cost.
  3. Prioritization by AI payback. We match processes to measured AI effects: support payroll costs down 30–40%, document processing 70–80% cheaper, decisions 20–40% faster. We rank candidates by payback period.
  4. AI strategy (TO BE). A document with the target state: what we automate, in what order, with which expected figures and risks.

What you get in hand

Three documents. A process map with bottlenecks, an economics table (what each process costs now and what it will cost with AI), and an AI strategy with rollout priorities for 6–12 months. The documents are self-contained: you can execute the plan without us, with any contractor.

Internal or external auditor

An internal audit is cheaper and knows context, but it has two weak spots: employees struggle to see familiar chaos from outside, and a department head struggles to write that their processes are inefficient. An external auditor looks without politics and compares you with practice at other companies. The working scheme is external method and calculation plus your internal process knowledge.

Free audit of one process or full audit

Start with a free audit of one process: you describe the task, we return an economics calculation and an honest conclusion on whether AI pays back there. That is enough to judge the method and the scale of value. A full audit covers all key company processes and ends with an AI strategy. Per surveys, only 26% of Russian companies have an AI strategy; the rest roll out pointwise and often land in failure stats: 30–40% of AI pilots are shut down without effect.
